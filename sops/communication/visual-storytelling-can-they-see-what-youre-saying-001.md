# Visual Storytelling: Can They See What You're Saying?

## Metadata
- **Source**: Nancy Duarte - Lenny's Podcast
- **Domain**: Communication/Presentation Design
- **Type**: Process
- **Applicable To**: Product Managers, Presenters, Team Leaders, Designers, Anyone communicating complex ideas
- **Difficulty**: Beginner to Intermediate

## Overview
A principle and practice for ensuring your audience can visualize your ideas through intentional use of visuals, diagrams, demonstrations, and verbal imagery. The core question to ask yourself: "Can they see what I'm saying?" This applies whether you're using slides, whiteboards, napkin sketches, or purely verbal descriptions.

## When to Use
- When explaining complex systems, architectures, or processes
- When presenting product features or technical concepts
- When trying to get team alignment on strategy or vision
- When you notice confused faces or repeated questions
- Before creating any presentation or communication
- When verbal explanation alone hasn't created understanding
- During product reviews or design critiques

## Prerequisites
- Clear understanding of what you're trying to communicate
- Ability to identify the core concept or structure you need to convey
- Willingness to draw, sketch, or create visuals (even crude ones)

## Procedure

### Step 1: Self-Assessment - Ask "Can They See What I'm Saying?"
Before and during communication, constantly evaluate visualization.
- As you prepare, review each point and ask: "Could someone draw this from my description?"
- During delivery, watch for signs people are visualizing (nodding, eye tracking, engagement)
- After key points, check: "Can you picture what I'm describing?"
- If the answer is no, shift to visual mode immediately

**Red flags that they can't see it**:
- Confused expressions
- Repeated questions about the same concept
- People asking "Can you draw that?"
- Lack of engagement or blank stares
- Misalignment in follow-up conversations

### Step 2: Choose the Right Visualization Method
Match the visualization approach to your context and content.

#### Option A: Presentation Slides with Strong Visuals
- **When**: Formal presentations, larger audiences, prepared talks
- **How**: Use diagrams, photos, minimal text
- **Key principle**: One visual concept per slide
- Use full-bleed images that convey emotion or concept
- Create diagrams that show relationships, flows, hierarchies
- Avoid dense text slides (use slide docs for that)

#### Option B: Live Drawing/Whiteboarding
- **When**: Meetings, workshops, real-time collaboration
- **How**: Stand up, go to whiteboard, draw while explaining
- **Key principle**: Co-create the visual with the audience
- Ask: "Let me draw what I'm seeing - is this your intent?"
- Build the diagram step-by-step as you narrate
- Invite others to add to or correct the drawing

#### Option C: Napkin Sketches
- **When**: Informal conversations, dinners, coffee chats, 1-on-1s
- **How**: Grab whatever's available, draw simple diagrams
- **Key principle**: Crude drawings are fine if they create shared understanding
- Focus on structure and relationships, not artistic quality
- Both parties can see and point to the same thing

#### Option D: Verbal Visualization (No Visual Aids)
- **When**: No tools available, or intentionally leaving slides off
- **How**: Paint pictures with words using vivid, concrete language
- **Key principle**: Create images in the mind's eye
- Use specific details, sensory language, analogies
- Reference familiar images or scenes
- Example: MLK's "I Have a Dream" - no slides, but everyone could see it

#### Option E: Physical Demonstrations
- **When**: Presenting products, user experiences, interactions
- **How**: Show the actual thing or walk through it live
- **Key principle**: Let them see it in action, not just described
- Live product demos
- User journey walkthroughs
- Physical prototypes or mockups

### Step 3: Design Visuals That Support Comprehension
When creating visual aids, follow these principles:

#### For Diagrams and Models
- **Show relationships**: Is X inside Y, outside Y, connected to Y, above Y?
- **Show flow**: Use arrows to indicate direction, sequence, causation
- **Show hierarchy**: Use size, position, or containment to show structure
- **Label clearly**: Don't assume people know what boxes or circles mean
- **Build progressively**: Reveal complex diagrams step-by-step

**Example use cases**:
- Product architecture: "This component sits between the API and database"
- Process flow: "Request comes in here, gets validated, then routed"
- Strategy framework: "These three pillars support our North Star metric"

#### For Data Visualization
- **Use dramatic data**: Big numbers displayed prominently
- **Create scale**: Show comparison to make numbers meaningful
- **Visual hierarchy**: Most important data point should be most prominent
- **Cinematic approach**: Let data fill the screen, create impact
- **Example**: Nancy's story of "BIG" in hot pink filling entire screen to light up faces

#### For Concept Illustration
- **One concept per slide**: Don't make people process multiple ideas
- **Use evocative imagery**: Photos or illustrations that create emotional connection
- **Consider full-bleed**: Let powerful images fill the entire slide
- **Avoid generic clip art**: Unless it's truly clarifying

### Step 4: Create "Star Moments" - Unforgettable Visual Moments
Build in specific moments designed to be permanently memorable.

**Types of star moments**:
- **Dramatic data**: Huge numbers, shocking statistics, compelling comparisons
- **Evocative story**: Narrative with vivid visual details
- **Beautiful picture**: Image that captures the essence of your message
- **Unexpected visual**: Something that breaks pattern and demands attention

**Example from Apple**: The word "BIG" in magenta pink on black background, bright enough to light the faces in the audience - created a gasp and became unforgettable.

### Step 5: Test and Validate Shared Understanding
Ensure alignment through visual confirmation.

**In team meetings**:
- After explaining, ask someone to draw what they understood
- Have the team co-create a visual of the plan/strategy
- Ensure everyone leaves with the same mental model
- Take a photo of the whiteboard and share it

**In presentations**:
- Watch body language for signs of visualization
- Note when people lean forward or nod (they're seeing it)
- Follow up afterward: "When you picture X, what do you see?"

**For critical alignment**:
- Create a diagram everyone must reference
- Use the same visual in all subsequent conversations
- Make it accessible (on wall, in shared docs)
- Example: Airbnb's customer journey frames in the center of the office

### Step 6: Know When to Turn Visuals Off
Understand when slides/visuals become a barrier rather than aid.

**Turn off slides when**:
- You want deep eye contact and personal connection
- The moment requires emotional authenticity
- You're petitioning someone powerful (sit side by side, not across from screen)
- Your words are powerful enough to paint the picture
- Visual aids would create distance between you and audience

**Example**: Nancy's client pitching CEO for $100M - advised to skip slides, use mental model he could explain face-to-face, with occasional whiteboard sketches for key moments. Result: Funded.

## Expected Outcomes
- Faster comprehension and reduced need for re-explanation
- Teams aligned on complex concepts with shared mental models
- Reduced misunderstandings and downstream confusion
- More memorable presentations and ideas
- Increased engagement and buy-in
- Ability to move forward without lingering questions

## Common Pitfalls
- **Assuming people see what's in your mind's eye**: You may see the full mosaic, but they only see a few tiles. Draw the complete picture.
- **Over-relying on text**: Words on slides rarely create visualization. Use diagrams and images instead.
- **Overly complex visuals**: If one diagram shows 15 relationships, break it into steps.
- **Poor drawing skills stopping you**: Crude drawings that create alignment are better than no drawing at all.
- **Using slides when you shouldn't**: High-stakes, personal moments often need slides OFF, not ON.
- **Generic stock photos**: Random images don't help visualization if they're not truly connected to concept.
- **Forgetting your audience can't see inside your head**: What's obvious to you is often invisible to them.
- **Not checking for understanding**: Ask "Can you see what I mean?" and actually wait for the answer.

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: CEO Who Couldn't Get Traction (Nancy's Client)
**Problem**: Multinational retail CEO had program for 100,000 workers, but couldn't get buy-in. People couldn't understand her vision.
**Solution**: Nancy's team asked "What's the picture in your mind's eye?" and drew it out while the CEO confirmed. Created a single graphic that captured the entire program.
**Result**: Massive breakthrough. Once people could see what she was saying, the program gained full traction.

### Example 2: Airbnb Customer Journey (Referenced by Lenny)
**Approach**: Hired Pixar storyboard artist to draw key frames of guest and host journey
**Visual**: 12 frames illustrating the complete experience
**Implementation**: Placed in center of office where anyone could grab a frame
**Impact**: Became the company strategy - "Pick 6 frames and make them awesome"
**Key**: Made the abstract strategy concrete and visible to everyone

### Example 3: Nancy's Presentation Designer Process
**Situation**: Client talking about complex concept using only hand gestures
**Action**: Designer stands up mid-conversation: "I can tell you have a picture in your mind's eye. Let me draw what I see..."
**Result**: Draws the structure, client confirms "Exactly!" - alignment achieved
**Outcome**: Whole room now sees the same thing, can move forward together

### Example 4: Apple "Think Different" Campaign
**Challenge**: Designer creating slides for Think Different campaign during era of beveled, cluttered backgrounds
**Problem**: Initial designs didn't match brand elegance
**Solution**: Nancy found stock video of floating particulates (like Hitchcock posters), used as subtle background with 6-color Apple logo
**Impact**: Revolutionary for the time, pushed the medium forward, maintained brand integrity

## AI Integration Notes

### Context Signals That Trigger This SOP
- User is preparing presentation materials
- User mentions audience confusion or lack of understanding
- User describing complex technical or strategic concepts
- User asking how to explain something more clearly
- User preparing for product reviews, architecture discussions, or strategy sessions
- User mentions "people don't seem to get it"

### How to Adapt for Different Situations
- **For technical audiences**: Favor detailed diagrams showing architecture, flow, and relationships; less emotional imagery
- **For executive audiences**: Use simple, high-level visuals; focus on strategic frameworks and dramatic data
- **For creative teams**: Use evocative imagery, mood boards, visual references; more aesthetic considerations
- **For remote presentations**: Ensure visuals are screen-readable; consider digital whiteboard tools
- **For in-person workshops**: Emphasize live whiteboarding and co-creation of visuals
- **For written communication (slide docs)**: Combine visuals with more text than you'd use in live presentation

### Limitations and Edge Cases
- Some concepts resist visualization (abstract philosophy, emotions) - use metaphor and analogy
- Cultural differences in visual interpretation - test understanding across cultures
- Accessibility considerations - always describe visuals verbally for those who can't see them
- Over-visualization can clutter and confuse - sometimes less is more
- Live drawing requires confidence - practice beforehand if anxious
- Not all moments need visuals - intimate, emotional moments may need slides OFF
- Production quality expectations vary by context - know your audience's standards
