# Manager to Manager-of-Managers Transition: Scaling Your Time and Influence

## Metadata
- **Source**: Camille Fournier - Lenny's Podcast
- **Domain**: Engineering Leadership
- **Type**: Framework
- **Applicable To**: Engineering Managers, Engineering Directors, Senior Leaders
- **Difficulty**: Advanced

## Overview
A framework for transitioning from managing individual contributors to managing managers, focusing on the critical shift from linear time management to scaled influence. This SOP addresses the most common failure mode: trying to maintain one-on-one relationships with everyone as your organization grows.

## When to Use
- When you're promoted from engineering manager to director or senior manager
- When your span of control expands beyond 8-10 direct reports
- When you find yourself overwhelmed with one-on-one meetings
- When designing management structures for growing engineering teams

## Prerequisites
- Experience as a first-line engineering manager
- Growing team or promotion to manager-of-managers role
- Frustration with current meeting load or time management
- Understanding of basic management practices (1:1s, feedback, etc.)

## Procedure

### Step 1: Recognize the Scaling Problem
Understand that one-on-one meetings don't scale linearly with organizational growth.

**The Math of Non-Scalability**:
- Direct reports: 1:1s weekly or biweekly (necessary and sacred)
- Your manager: 1:1s weekly or biweekly (necessary and sacred)
- Peers across the organization: Cannot all be 1:1s
- Stakeholders (product, design, other teams): Cannot all be 1:1s
- Cross-functional partners: Cannot all be 1:1s

**The Trap**: As your company/team grows, the number of relationships grows exponentially, but time remains fixed. You cannot scale by adding 1:1s for every new relationship.

### Step 2: Audit Your Current Meeting Load
Conduct an honest assessment of where your time is going.

**Meeting Audit Categories**:
1. **Sacred 1:1s** (keep these):
   - Your direct reports
   - Your manager

2. **Stakeholder 1:1s** (evaluate these):
   - Product managers
   - Design leads
   - Peer engineering managers
   - Cross-functional partners

3. **Relationship 1:1s** (probably cut these):
   - Networking coffee chats
   - "Let's stay in touch" meetings
   - Informational meetings without clear purpose

**Question to Ask**: "Do I really have something to talk to this person about, or am I just collecting 1:1s to feel productive?"

### Step 3: Respect Your Time (and Others' Time)
Develop a principled approach to meeting requests.

**The Time Respect Framework**:
- **Before requesting a 1:1**: Ask yourself what specific topic or decision requires this meeting
- **If there's no clear purpose**: Don't schedule it just because "good corporate citizens have 1:1s"
- **Recognize**: You are asking for someone's time, not just filling your calendar
- **Remember**: 8 meetings in a day ≠ productivity

**Warning Signs You're Not Respecting Time**:
- Loading your calendar just because you're a manager
- Accepting every meeting request by default
- Feeling productive because your calendar is full
- Unable to articulate the purpose of upcoming 1:1s

### Step 4: Replace Some 1:1s with Group Forums
Shift from individual conversations to group settings for certain types of communication.

**When Group Forums Work Better**:

**Scenario 1: Platform Team with Multiple Stakeholders**
- **Problem**: You have 10 internal stakeholders who all want 1:1s
- **1:1 Approach**: 10 separate meetings where happy stakeholders say "it's fine" and unhappy ones complain
- **Group Approach**: Monthly stakeholder forum where all stakeholders hear each other
- **Benefit**: Unhappy stakeholder hears directly from 5 happy stakeholders, rather than you saying "trust me, everyone else is happy"

**Scenario 2: Cross-Team Coordination**
- **Problem**: Multiple teams need to stay aligned on shared infrastructure
- **1:1 Approach**: Sequential 1:1s where you play telephone between teams
- **Group Approach**: Weekly cross-team sync with all relevant parties
- **Benefit**: Direct communication, shared context, faster decisions

**Scenario 3: Peer Manager Alignment**
- **Problem**: Need to coordinate with 5 peer engineering managers
- **1:1 Approach**: 5 separate 1:1s repeating the same information
- **Group Approach**: Weekly engineering managers sync
- **Benefit**: Information shared once, collaborative problem-solving

### Step 5: Know Your Strengths and Lean Out of Comfort Zone
Understand your natural tendencies and deliberately work against them.

**Self-Assessment**:

**If You're Naturally Good at Relationship Building**:
- You excel at: Random coffee chats, networking, building rapport without agenda
- Your weakness: May be using 1:1s as your comfort zone
- **Action**: Push yourself to do FEWER relationship 1:1s
- **Why**: You should always be getting out of your comfort zone

**If You're Not Great at Company Networking**:
- You excel at: Deep collaboration on specific problems
- Your weakness: Building relationships without clear work purpose
- **Action**: You might need to do MORE strategic relationship 1:1s
- **Why**: Balance your natural task-focus with relationship building

**Camille's Self-Awareness**:
"I'm really bad at just getting to know you random one-on-one where we don't have a purpose to the meeting. I'm really good if I work with you. If I work with you on something, people really come to respect me because I'm very engaged and a really good collaborator."

### Step 6: Collaborate on Real Work to Build Relationships
Recognize that working together is often more valuable than talking about working together.

**Relationship Building Through Collaboration**:
- **Traditional Approach**: Schedule 1:1 → Build relationship → Eventually work together
- **Effective Approach**: Work together on real problem → Relationship forms naturally → Mutual respect develops

**How to Apply**:
- When someone requests a 1:1, ask: "What are we trying to solve?"
- If there's a real problem, offer to collaborate on solving it
- Skip the "getting to know you" phase and build trust through work
- Reserve purely social 1:1s for strategic relationships

### Step 7: Implement the One-on-One Reduction Framework
Systematically reduce and optimize your 1:1 load.

**Decision Matrix for Each 1:1**:

| Relationship Type | Frequency | Format | Justification |
|-------------------|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Direct reports | Weekly or biweekly | 1:1 | Sacred - non-negotiable |
| Your manager | Weekly or biweekly | 1:1 | Sacred - non-negotiable |
| Critical stakeholder on active project | Biweekly during project | 1:1 | Time-bounded, specific purpose |
| Multiple stakeholders on same topic | Monthly | Group forum | More efficient, better transparency |
| Peer wanting to "stay aligned" | As-needed | Slack/Group meeting | Async or group unless specific issue |
| Networking/relationship building | Quarterly or less | Optional | Only if strategic value is clear |

**Implementation Steps**:
1. List all recurring 1:1s on your calendar
2. Categorize each using the matrix above
3. Identify 1:1s to cancel, reduce frequency, or convert to group settings
4. Communicate changes proactively: "I'm consolidating stakeholder updates into a monthly forum—would love to see you there"

### Step 8: Delegate Relationship Management
Empower your managers to own relationships at their level.

**What to Delegate**:
- **Peer engineer relationships**: Your engineering managers should handle relationships with peer teams' managers
- **Technical stakeholder details**: Let senior engineers own specific technical partnerships
- **Day-to-day coordination**: Your managers should solve coordination problems without escalating to you

**What to Keep**:
- **Executive relationships**: You own relationships with VPs, C-suite
- **Strategic partnerships**: Cross-org initiatives that require director-level buy-in
- **Escalations**: When your managers have tried and need your level of authority

## Expected Outcomes
- **More strategic time**: Calendar opens up for thinking, planning, and high-leverage work
- **Empowered managers**: Your direct reports grow by owning relationships
- **Better transparency**: Group forums create shared understanding across stakeholders
- **Reduced burnout**: Sustainable meeting load that doesn't require 60+ hour weeks
- **Maintained relationships**: Key relationships stay strong through quality over quantity

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: The "I Should Have a 1:1 with Everyone" Trap
**Problem**: Believing good managers meet one-on-one with every person in their sphere of influence.

**How to Avoid**:
- Recognize this literally doesn't scale past 15-20 relationships
- Do the math: 20 biweekly 1:1s = 10 hours/week just in 1:1s, plus prep time
- Accept that group settings are sometimes more effective

### Pitfall 2: Using 1:1s to Avoid Group Conflict
**Problem**: Hiding in 1:1s to avoid facilitating difficult group conversations.

**How to Avoid**:
- If all your stakeholders are saying the same thing in 1:1s, that should be a group conversation
- Learn facilitation skills for difficult group dynamics
- Recognize that individual conversations can enable people to avoid accountability

### Pitfall 3: Mistaking Calendar Fullness for Productivity
**Problem**: Feeling productive because you had 8 meetings today.

**How to Avoid**:
- Ask: "What decisions did I make today? What did I unblock?"
- Measure output (decisions, progress, problems solved) not input (meetings attended)
- Build in thinking/writing time as "productive" calendar time

### Pitfall 4: Remote Work Meeting Proliferation
**Problem**: Remote work makes it easier to add "quick" 1:1s that accumulate quickly.

**How to Avoid**:
- Set a hard cap on weekly 1:1s (e.g., max 15 including sacred ones)
- Use Slack/async communication more aggressively
- Create group channels for topics that don't need synchronous time

### Pitfall 5: Not Investing in Delegation Skills
**Problem**: Continuing to own all relationships because you haven't taught your managers to own them.

**How to Avoid**:
- Explicitly coach your managers on stakeholder management
- Introduce your managers to key stakeholders, then step back
- Resist the urge to jump into every relationship problem

## Advanced Techniques

### Technique 1: The Stakeholder Forum
**Setup**:
- Monthly 60-minute meeting with all key stakeholders for your platform/area
- Standing agenda: Updates, priorities, Q&A, feedback
- Rotate spotlight on different stakeholders to share their needs

**Benefits**:
- Unhappy stakeholders hear from happy ones directly
- Creates peer accountability and transparency
- Reduces your role as "translator" between stakeholders

### Technique 2: Office Hours
**Setup**:
- Block 2-3 hours per week as "office hours"
- Anyone can grab 15-30 minutes without a formal 1:1 request
- Advertise in team channels

**Benefits**:
- Accessible without the commitment of recurring 1:1s
- Issues get addressed when they're timely
- Reduces formal meeting load

### Technique 3: The 6-Month 1:1 Reset
**Setup**:
- Every 6 months, cancel all non-sacred recurring 1:1s
- Ask each person: "Do we still need to meet regularly? What's the purpose?"
- Only reinstate 1:1s with clear ongoing value

**Benefits**:
- Prevents 1:1 accumulation over time
- Forces explicit value assessment
- Creates opportunity to shift to group forums

**How to Adapt for Different Situations**:

- **For extroverted relationship builders**: Push harder on the "lean out of comfort zone" advice
- **For remote teams**: Acknowledge async communication is even more important
- **For platform/infrastructure teams**: Emphasize stakeholder forum approach heavily

**Limitations and Edge Cases**:
- Some company cultures expect high meeting volume (may need to navigate politically)
