# AI-First Product Transformation

## Metadata
- **Source**: Tomer Cohen (CPO, LinkedIn) - Lenny's Podcast
- **Domain**: Product Management & Strategy
- **Type**: Framework
- **Applicable To**: Product Leaders, CPOs, VPs of Product at scale companies (200+ people)
- **Difficulty**: Advanced

## Overview
A comprehensive framework for transforming an organization from traditional product development to an AI-first mindset, where AI becomes the core engine rather than an add-on feature. This approach reorganizes teams, processes, and thinking to leverage AI as "the ultimate matchmaker" in marketplace and platform products.

## When to Use
- When transitioning from traditional product development to AI-powered experiences
- When AI has been treated as a separate function rather than core to product strategy
- When building marketplace or platform products that need sophisticated matching
- When experiencing organizational confusion about how to integrate AI effectively
- Before gen AI/LLM waves arrive (preparation phase)

## Prerequisites
- Executive-level support for organizational change
- Existing AI/ML team (even if siloed)
- Clear product objectives and metrics
- Willingness to reorganize team structures
- Understanding of your product's core value exchange

## Procedure

### Step 1: Shift Mindset from Technology to Strategy
**Objective**: Make AI a strategic consideration, not just a technical one

- Start every strategy discussion with "what role does AI play in this?"
- Frame AI as a mindset, not a technology stack
- Use the river rafting analogy: "AI is the two paddles at the back that steer the boat - you (PM) must hold them"
- Establish that product leaders, not engineers, own AI direction
- Create belief that AI is the biggest technological revolution in our lifetimes

**Key Principle**: "It wasn't like 'Oh, we have this cool technology, what can we do with it?' It was like 'Let go of what you've built. Go back to the objectives you were trying to solve and now with this technology, how can you do that objective better?'"

### Step 2: Unify AI with Product Teams
**Objective**: Break down silos between AI teams and product teams

- Identify where your AI team sits (typically centralized and separate)
- Create a unified "AI-first team" with one set of objectives
- Bring AI engineers, data scientists, and product managers into one organizational unit
- Establish that the PM/product leader spends most of their time on:
  - Algorithm objectives
  - Algorithm features (parameters to learn on)
  - Data collection strategy
  - Fine-tuning investments

**Example**: LinkedIn brought the centralized AI team together with the Feed product team into one unified SWAT team with aligned objectives.

### Step 3: Educate Product Leaders on AI Fundamentals
**Objective**: Make every PM an "AI Product Leader"

Establish an AI Academy that covers:

**Core Knowledge Areas**:
- Algorithm objectives: What is the mathematical formula your algorithm optimizes for?
- Algorithm features: What parameters does it learn on? (not user features)
- Data collection: What training data do you need and how will you get it?
- Fine-tuning: How will you continuously improve the model?
- Infrastructure: How does infrastructure impact product outcomes?

**Training Approach**:
- Every PM must complete AI training (similar to mobile-first training)
- Spend significant review time on AI strategy in product reviews
- Hire AI practitioners on the product side who can teach
- Build distinguished leaders across functions who can spread knowledge
- Create product jams focused entirely on AI strategy

**Assessment**: Product leaders should be able to write the algorithm objective on a whiteboard and explain every parameter

### Step 4: Revisit Entire Roadmap Through AI Lens
**Objective**: Systematically rethink everything with AI capabilities

**Process** (Fall 2022 example):
1. Call all product leaders together
2. Ask them to "let go of your roadmaps"
3. Go back to original problem statements (not solutions)
4. Meet in 2 weeks with new solutions leveraging AI
5. Allow divergence phase: Let teams explore and build duplicates
6. Learn what works through experimentation (weeks of exploration)
7. Converge phase: Top-down selection of 4-5 biggest bets
8. Weekly product jams on only those priority bets

**Key Insight**: This requires letting go of control. "You don't control the experience anymore, you control the ingredients."

### Step 5: Focus on Objectives, Not Features
**Objective**: Ensure AI optimizes for the right outcomes

**For Marketplace Products**:
- Define both sides of the marketplace
- Specify the value exchange (not just volume)
- Trade off bad engagement for quality matches
- Example: LinkedIn shifted from click-through to downstream conversations

**For Each Product**:
- Specify downstream metrics (not just top-funnel)
- Define "active, engaged, and high-value engagement"
- Put onus on meaningful outcomes
- Build objectives around value creation, not traffic generation

### Step 6: Implement the AI-First Tech Stack
**Three Components**:

**1. Platform**:
- Re-architect core platforms so AI can reason over them
- Build composable UI components with server-side capability
- Create AI-ready design systems
- Invest before expecting returns

**2. Tools & Agents**:
- Build specialized agents for each function (trust, growth, research, analyst)
- Train on your unique context, not generic data
- Customize third-party tools (Copilot, Cursor) to work with your codebase
- Create orchestration layer between agents

**3. Culture**:
- Change performance review criteria to include AI fluency
- Celebrate wins publicly
- Share tools and learnings regularly
- Create FOMO through limited access initially
- Require agency and willingness to make tools better

### Step 7: Carve Out Safe Spaces for Transformation
**Objective**: Prove the model works without breaking existing metrics

**Approach**:
- Select a controlled cohort (e.g., 2 million users out of larger base)
- Declare this "your members" to build for
- Give full liberty to experiment without impacting overall metrics
- Run for months to see behavioral change
- Use results as proof point for organization-wide rollout

**Rationale**: At scale companies, every change scares teams dependent on existing metrics. A carve-out prevents escalation fatigue.

### Step 8: Invest in Data Quality, Not Just Quantity
**Objective**: Train AI on the right examples

- Don't just give agents access to your entire drive/database
- Curate "golden examples" of great work
- Manually filter through weeks of samples
- Weight importance of historical examples
- Clean up corpus before training
- Tie examples to original specs and success metrics

**Key Learning**: "Just reasoning over your entire knowledge base did not work."

### Step 9: Measure and Communicate Progress
**Objective**: Maintain momentum and transparency

**Metrics to Track**:
- Time saved per person per week
- Quality of insights and discussions
- Experiment volume ร quality รท time to launch
- Adoption rates of AI tools
- Top talent engagement with new tools

**Communication**:
- Regular all-hands on tool usage
- Showcase examples of wins
- Share learnings from failures
- Over-communicate the vision
- Hold yourself accountable with public OKRs

## Expected Outcomes
- Product development becomes 2-5x faster for AI-enabled features
- Quality of matching/recommendations improves dramatically
- Organization develops competitive advantage in AI capabilities
- Product leaders can articulate AI strategy clearly
- Teams can respond to AI waves (like GPT/LLMs) within weeks, not quarters
- Top talent becomes more engaged and productive

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Treating AI as a Feature Team
**Problem**: Keeping AI centralized and separate from product teams
**Solution**: Unify AI and product into one team with one set of objectives

### Pitfall 2: Delegating AI to Engineering
**Problem**: Product leaders say "I don't understand AI, the engineers handle it"
**Solution**: Product leaders must learn AI fundamentals and own algorithm objectives

### Pitfall 3: Starting with Tech, Not Problems
**Problem**: "We have this cool AI, what should we build?"
**Solution**: Start with clear problem statements, then apply AI to solve them better

### Pitfall 4: Giving Up Control Too Slowly
**Problem**: Trying to maintain deterministic control over AI experiences
**Solution**: Accept that AI is non-deterministic; focus on ingredients and guardrails

### Pitfall 5: Insufficient Cultural Investment
**Problem**: Launching tools without change management
**Solution**: Invest heavily in training, incentives, examples, and celebration

### Pitfall 6: Rushing Organization-Wide Rollout
**Problem**: Forcing transformation before proving it works
**Solution**: Use controlled cohorts to prove the model, then expand

### Pitfall 7: Not Investing in Platform
**Problem**: Expecting third-party tools to work off-the-shelf
**Solution**: Re-architect platforms to be AI-ready; customize all tools

### Pitfall 8: Treating Fine-Tuning as Engineering Work
**Problem**: Assuming engineers will handle data collection and fine-tuning
**Solution**: Make it a product strategy priority; whole organization owns it

## Real-World Example: LinkedIn Feed Transformation

**Context**: LinkedIn feed was promotional, low engagement, seen as traffic source for other products

**Application**:
1. Created first unified Feed team with dedicated PM (Tomer himself)
2. Brought AI team into unified structure with Feed team
3. Reset purpose: "People that matter, talking about things I care about professionally"
4. Carved out 2M member cohort for experimentation
5. Shifted AI objective from clicks to downstream conversations
6. Invested in removing spam as AI got better
7. Result: Multi-day active user product, creator economy thriving

## AI Integration Notes

### Context Signals That Should Trigger This SOP
- Product development velocity declining despite growing team
- AI being discussed as add-on features rather than core strategy
- Multiple teams building AI capabilities independently
- Leadership confusion about "what to do with AI"
- New AI waves (like GPT-4) causing reactive scrambling
- Marketplace/platform products struggling with relevance/matching

### How to Adapt for Different Situations

**For Smaller Companies (10-50 people)**:
- Skip the carve-out step; can move faster org-wide
- Combine Steps 2-3 (everyone should be in one team anyway)
- Focus more on Step 4 (roadmap revisit)
- May not need formal AI Academy; can do team workshops

**For Non-Marketplace Products**:
- Focus less on "matching" language
- Adapt Step 5 to focus on user journey optimization
- Consider personalization vs. matching as the core value

**For B2B vs B2C**:
- B2B: Emphasize trust agent and compliance considerations
- B2C: Emphasize scale, engagement quality, and safety

### Limitations and Edge Cases
- Requires minimum team size of ~50-100 to justify reorganization
- Less applicable to pure hardware or non-software products
- May face resistance in highly regulated industries (though still valuable)
- Assumes availability of AI/ML talent to hire
- Timeline: 6-12 months minimum to see full transformation

### Signs of Success
- Product leaders can explain algorithm objectives without hesitation
- Teams propose AI-first solutions by default
- New features ship faster than before transformation
- Top talent requests to join "AI-first" teams
- Company can rapidly respond to new AI capabilities (weeks, not quarters)

### Warning Signs
- Only 5% of team engaging with new approaches
- Continued siloing of AI team
- Product reviews not discussing AI strategy
- Roadmaps looking same as pre-transformation
- Heavy reliance on third-party tools without customization
