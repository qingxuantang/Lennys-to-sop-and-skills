# Avoiding Tarpit Ideas: Identifying Deceptively Attractive Bad Ideas

## Metadata
- **Source**: Dalton Caldwell - Lenny's Podcast
- **Domain**: Strategy / Idea Evaluation
- **Type**: Framework + Checklist
- **Applicable To**: Pre-seed founders, idea-stage entrepreneurs, startup advisors
- **Difficulty**: Intermediate

## Overview
A framework for identifying "tarpit ideas" - startup concepts that seem compelling, receive positive validation, and attract many founders, but consistently fail. Unlike obviously bad ideas, tarpits are dangerous because they draw you in with encouraging signals before trapping you in an unwinnable situation.

## When to Use
- Evaluating a new startup idea before committing to it
- Conducting idea diligence before applying to accelerators
- Deciding whether to pivot away from current idea
- Advising founders on idea selection
- Researching a problem space and seeing many failed attempts
- Getting lots of positive feedback but no actual traction

## Prerequisites
- Basic understanding of startup validation
- Ability to research past attempts in a space
- Willingness to abandon ideas that fit tarpit pattern
- Understanding that positive feedback ≠ validation

## Procedure

### Step 1: Understand What Makes a Tarpit (Not Just a Hard Idea)
Learn the defining characteristics of tarpits.

**Critical Distinction:**
- Regular hard idea: Difficult, might fail, but you know it's hard
- Tarpit idea: Seems like an unsolved problem + gets positive validation + consistently fails

**Key Quote:** "By definition it is only a tarpit if it seems like it's not. If it's just a regular idea that is hard, that is not a tarpit."

**Tarpit Characteristics:**
- Seems like an obviously good idea
- You can easily validate it with customer conversations
- Friends/potential users say "Yes, I would love that!"
- Many smart people have tried and failed
- Has been attempted "since the '90s" or for decades
- You have "a really good set of arguments that it's a really good startup idea"

### Step 2: Research Historical Attempts
Investigate how many times this has been tried before.
- Google: "[your idea] startup failed" or "[your idea] startup graveyard"
- Search AngelList/Crunchbase for similar companies (many shut down?)
- Check: How many YC companies have tried this? (YC company search)
- Look for pattern: Multiple well-funded attempts that all pivoted away
- Warning sign: "People have been starting that startup since the '90s"
- Ask mentors: "Have you seen this idea before?"

### Step 3: Examine the Validation Pattern
Distinguish between polite enthusiasm and genuine demand.
- Tarpit pattern: "You'll get all this positive feedback from the world"
- Question: Are people saying "cool idea!" or committing to use/pay?
- Test: "Would you pay for this?" vs. "Would you use this if it were free?"
- Reality check: Did they immediately pull out their credit card or ask when they can start?
- Example: Localmind (Lenny's story) - "Holy shit, this is the most incredible thing I've ever seen. I could see what's happening at this bar" → then they never use it again

### Step 4: Check Against Known Tarpit Categories
Compare against established tarpit patterns.

**Common Tarpit Ideas (from transcript):**
1. **Social coordination apps**: Apps to help friends decide where to meet up or hang out
   - Why tarpit: Everyone says they want this, then doesn't use it
   - Pattern: "Building an app to coordinate with your friends to decide where to go out at night"

2. **Music discovery**: Helping people find new music
   - Dalton's experience: "I worked on tarpit ideas myself as a founder, which is music discovery"
   - Why tarpit: Gets users initially, but hard to sustain/monetize

3. **Location-based social**: Foursquare clones and check-in apps
   - Historical: "Do you remember when Foursquare clones was all anyone worked on for years?"
   - Result: "All the Foursquare clones, if they didn't pivot out of doing what they're doing, wouldn't have worked"

4. **[Your research will reveal more categories]**

### Step 5: Apply the "Same Information Diet" Test
Assess if your idea is too derivative.
- Question: "If you're listening to the same podcast... reading the same people on Twitter... you're friends with all the same people. Does it seem surprising then that you would all end up with similar startup ideas?"
- Warning sign: Your idea came from reading tech news/Twitter/podcasts
- Counter-signal: Idea came from personal experience, deep hobby, or unique insight
- Goal: "Go more off the beaten path either from your personal experience"

### Step 6: Conduct the "Fashion Cycle" Check
Identify if this is currently trendy (danger zone).
- Pattern: "You'll see things that become fashionable really quickly because someone found success in this unfashionable space and then it becomes fashionable"
- Example: "A few years ago, startups around trucking were super new and fresh... then it became completely conventional wisdom to do trucking related startups"
- Warning: If everyone is suddenly doing [X] ideas, you're late and it might be a tarpit
- Reality: "People all have the same idea" when something becomes fashionable

### Step 7: Make Go/No-Go Decision
Decide whether to proceed or abandon idea.

**Proceed if:**
- Idea has tarpit qualities BUT you have unique insight others missed
- You've found a specific wedge/angle nobody else tried
- You have founder-market fit that others attempting this didn't have
- You can articulate WHY you'll succeed where others failed

**Abandon if:**
- Fits tarpit pattern AND you have no unique advantage
- You're getting positive feedback but no commitments
- Multiple well-funded attempts have failed for same reasons
- You can't explain your unique insight/advantage
- You chose it because it seems like an unsolved problem (that's the trap!)

### Step 8: Pivot Using "Warmth" Direction
If abandoning tarpit, move toward founder-market fit.
- Direction: "Go more off the beaten path... from your personal experience"
- Strategy: Find "marketplaces for Funko Pops" not "social coordination app"
- Approach: "Try to mix up what your information diet is or what areas of expertise you have and mine that well"
- Alternative: Find "publicly traded companies... that are large and that also are hated by their customers" (Rujul/Zip approach)

## Expected Outcomes
- Clear identification of whether your idea fits tarpit pattern
- Understanding of why idea seems good but will likely fail
- Historical context of previous attempts and why they failed
- Decision to either: find unique angle, or abandon for better idea
- Reduced wasted time building something destined to fail

## Common Pitfalls

**Believing "But We'll Execute Better"**
- Problem: Thinking good execution will overcome tarpit dynamics
- Why it fails: Previous attempts also had great execution; the problem is structural
- Solution: Need unique insight, not just better execution

**Mistaking Positive Feedback for Validation**
- Problem: "Everyone I talked to said they'd use it!"
- Why it fails: "Part of being a true tarpit is that you can get good initial validation"
- Solution: Push for commitments, pre-sales, actual usage - not just enthusiasm

**Thinking You're Different**
- Problem: "Yes, many have tried, but I have [minor advantage]"
- Why it fails: Unless advantage is truly unique, you'll hit same walls
- Solution: Honest assessment - is your advantage structural or incremental?

**Ignoring the Pattern**
- Problem: "Well, someone will eventually solve this"
- Why it fails: Maybe true, but odds are very low it will be you without unique edge
- Solution: Unless you have exceptional insight why now is different, move on

**Getting Trapped by Sunk Cost**
- Problem: "We've already built so much, we can't give up now"
- Why it fails: "You get stuck because it seems like it's a good idea and you get all this positive feedback"
- Solution: Recognize the tarpit early and pivot before investing more

## AI Integration Notes

### Context Signals That Should Trigger This SOP
- User describes idea that gets great feedback but no traction
- User asks about ideas in social/coordination/discovery spaces
- User mentions "everyone says they'd use this"
- User is working on idea many others have tried
- User describes fashionable/trendy idea space
- User frustrated that validation doesn't match results

### How to Adapt for Different Situations
- **First-Time Founders**: Emphasize learning the pattern early to save years
- **Technical Founders**: Warn that building ability doesn't overcome tarpit dynamics
- **Experienced Founders**: Acknowledge they might have unique insight, but verify it
- **Advisors/Investors**: Use as filter to steer founders away from tarpits

### Questions to Ask Users
1. "How many other companies have tried something similar in the past 5 years?"
2. "Are people giving you polite enthusiasm or pulling out their credit cards?"
3. "What unique insight do you have that previous attempts didn't?"
4. "Did this idea come from personal experience or from reading tech news?"
5. "If you describe the problem, do people say 'yes, someone should solve that!' (danger sign)"

### Red Flags to Watch For
- User gets defensive when you ask about previous attempts
- Can't name any unique advantage beyond "better execution"
- Idea came from seeing another startup get funded
- Describes positive customer validation but zero paying customers
- Has been working on it for months with great feedback but no growth
- Keeps adding features hoping one will drive adoption

### Exceptions and Nuances
- Platform shifts (mobile, AI) can make old tarpits viable (but verify!)
- Sometimes a tarpit with the right business model can work (Spotify for music)
- Occasionally someone with exceptional insight does solve a tarpit (rare!)
- B2B versions of consumer tarpits sometimes work (different dynamics)

### Key Dalton Quotes to Reference
- "By definition it is only a tarpit if it seems like it's not"
- "You'll get all this positive feedback from the world. And man, people have been starting that startup since the '90s"
- "That's a tarpit, is just something that's super appealing and a lot of people do it. And then you can get validation and that's why is a tarpit, is it draws you in and you get stuck"
- "People all have the same idea" when they have the same information diet
- "Try to go more off the beaten path either from your personal experience"

### Historical Examples to Reference
- **Foursquare clones**: Everyone built them, all failed except Foursquare (which pivoted to B2B)
- **Music discovery**: Countless attempts, very few successes despite obvious need
- **Social coordination**: Persistent idea since early internet, consistent failures
- **Trucking startups**: Were fresh, became trendy, now likely oversaturated
- **Localmind** (Lenny's example): Amazing demo, zero retention
